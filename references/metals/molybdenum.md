# Molybdenum (Mo) — Evaluation Notes

**Rarely a standalone deposit — almost always evaluate it as a by-product/co-product first.** The large majority of molybdenum comes from porphyry copper-molybdenum systems (Mo as a by-product credit of copper mining); primary/standalone porphyry molybdenum deposits exist but are a minority of global supply. Before treating a project's Mo content as a major independent value driver, establish which case you're in.

**Deposit types**: porphyry Cu-Mo (by far the most common — see `references/metals/copper.md`), and standalone porphyry Mo deposits (Climax/Henderson-style, associated with more evolved, silica-rich granitic intrusions rather than typical calc-alkaline porphyry copper systems).

**Grade reporting**: molybdenum grades are typically reported as % Mo or % MoS₂ (molybdenite) — confirm which basis is used, since MoS₂ contains ~60% Mo by mass and mixing up the two bases materially changes a contained-metal calculation.

**Processing**: recovered via flotation as a molybdenite (MoS₂) concentrate, typically as a separate concentrate stream from copper concentrate in Cu-Mo porphyries (produced via selective/differential flotation) — check the flowsheet shows Mo as its own concentrate product with its own recovery and payability terms, not folded into copper concentrate value.

**Market factors**: molybdenum pricing is more cyclical and less liquid/transparent than copper (traded via producer/merchant contracts referencing published price assessments rather than a major futures exchange) — treat a project's Mo price assumption with the same scrutiny as a minor-metal by-product credit, and check it's sourced from a specific dated reference rather than an unstated assumption. Demand is tied to steel alloying (high-strength steel, stainless steel) — moves with steel/infrastructure cycles more than with electrification narratives.

**Common evaluation pitfalls**: presenting Mo by-product revenue as if it materially de-risks a marginal copper project without checking the actual payability/recovery terms; conflating % Mo and % MoS₂ grade reporting.
