# Titanium (Ti) — Evaluation Notes

**Two distinct source types with different economics — identify which one first:**

**1. Mineral sands (ilmenite/rutile/leucoxene)**: the dominant global source, recovered from heavy mineral sand deposits alongside zircon (see `references/metals/zirconium.md` — read that file too, since most ilmenite/rutile projects are effectively co-products of a single HMS operation). Processing is the well-established gravity/magnetic/electrostatic separation chain described in the zirconium reference.

**2. Hard-rock titanium (massive ilmenite / titanomagnetite in mafic intrusions)**: a less common but real source (e.g., some Norwegian, Canadian, and South African deposits), typically requiring conventional crushing/milling and magnetic/gravity separation rather than the mineral-sands wet-plant chain — a materially different, generally higher-capex processing setup per tonne of ore than mineral sands.

**Grade reporting and product distinction — this matters more for titanium than for most commodities**: TiO₂ content is reported, but the *mineral* carrying it changes the product value enormously:
- **Rutile** (naturally high TiO₂, ~95%+) — the premium product, directly usable in the chloride process for pigment production and in welding-rod/titanium-metal applications; commands the highest price of the titanium feedstocks.
- **Ilmenite** (lower TiO₂, ~45–65%) — lower value as-is; can be upgraded via smelting to produce titania slag or synthetic rutile to substitute for natural rutile feedstock, which adds a processing step (and capex) but captures more value.
- **Leucoxene** (naturally altered/weathered ilmenite with elevated TiO₂) — intermediate value.

A project's headline "TiO₂ resource" is not comparable across projects without knowing this mineral split — a rutile-dominant resource is a fundamentally better story than an ilmenite-dominant one of the same nominal TiO₂ tonnage, unless the project specifically plans (and has costed) an upgrading circuit.

**Market factors**: titanium mineral demand is overwhelmingly driven by **TiO₂ pigment** production (paints, coatings, plastics, paper) via either the chloride process (needs high-TiO₂ feedstock — rutile or upgraded ilmenite) or the sulphate process (can use lower-grade ilmenite directly, more chemically intensive and generally a China/other-Asia-concentrated production route). A smaller share goes to titanium metal (aerospace, defense) via the Kroll process, which demands very high purity sponge-grade feedstock. Check which end-market and which process route a project's offtake actually targets — pigment-grade and metal-grade titanium feedstock are priced and qualified completely differently.

**Common evaluation pitfalls**: quoting a combined TiO₂ resource figure without disclosing the rutile/ilmenite/leucoxene split; assuming ilmenite can be sold at rutile-equivalent pricing without costing an upgrading circuit; not identifying whether the offtake target is the chloride-process pigment market, the sulphate-process pigment market, or the titanium-metal market, since these have different quality specs and price levels.
