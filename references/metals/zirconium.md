# Zirconium (Zr) — Evaluation Notes

**Almost always a mineral sands / heavy mineral sands (HMS) commodity, and almost always co-produced with titanium minerals** — zircon (ZrSiO₄) occurs alongside ilmenite and rutile (see `references/metals/titanium.md`) in heavy mineral sand deposits, so evaluate a zircon project's economics as one product stream within a broader HMS operation rather than in isolation, unless the resource statement clearly indicates a standalone zircon-dominant deposit (less common).

**Deposit type**: placer / heavy mineral sand deposits — concentrations of dense, weathering-resistant minerals (zircon, ilmenite, rutile, and sometimes monazite/xenotime carrying rare earths — see `references/metals/rare-earth.md`) in ancient or modern shoreline/dune/alluvial settings. Grade is typically reported as a combination of "total heavy mineral" (THM) % in the raw sand, and then the assemblage split among the individual heavy minerals (what % of THM is zircon vs. ilmenite vs. rutile, etc.) — always check both numbers; a high THM% resource with a low zircon fraction in the mineral assemblage may still be a poor zircon story even if it's a strong titanium-mineral story.

**Processing**: mining is typically low-cost dredging or dry mining of unconsolidated sand, followed by physical concentration (gravity spirals, then a "mineral separation plant" using electrostatic, magnetic, and gravity separation to split the heavy mineral concentrate into individual mineral products — zircon, ilmenite, rutile, and sometimes a monazite/rare-earth by-product stream). This is a well-established, relatively low-technical-risk processing chain by mining-industry standards, compared to e.g. HPAL nickel or REE separation — a useful contrast point when discussing technical risk.

**Market factors**: zircon demand is driven primarily by ceramics (zircon flour/opacifiers for tiles) and, to a lesser extent, refractories and zirconium chemicals/metal — a meaningfully different demand driver from the titanium minerals it's co-produced with (which serve the pigment/TiO₂ market — see `references/metals/titanium.md`). Because the two products serve different end markets, their prices don't necessarily move together, so a combined HMS project's revenue sensitivity should be checked against both product price decks independently, not treated as a single "mineral sands price."

**Common evaluation pitfalls**: evaluating zircon grade/value without reference to the full heavy mineral assemblage (THM% and speciation); assuming zircon and titanium mineral prices move in lockstep when modeling revenue sensitivity; overlooking a monazite/REE by-product stream that some HMS deposits carry, which can be a meaningful (and separately regulated, given thorium/uranium content in monazite) additional consideration.
