# Tungsten (W) — Evaluation Notes

*See `references/metals/scheelite.md` for the scheelite-specific case — the processing route differs enough from wolframite that it's kept as a separate reference rather than folded in here. Use this file for wolframite-hosted or mixed wolframite/scheelite deposits, and as the general tungsten market/strategic-context reference.*

**Supply concentration and strategic status**: one country (China) accounts for the large majority of global tungsten mine supply, making tungsten one of the more supply-concentrated critical/strategic metals — several governments (EU, US, Japan) classify it as a critical raw material. New tungsten projects outside China are frequently evaluated partly on supply-diversification and strategic-stockpile narrative, similar to niobium (see `references/metals/niobium-tantalum.md`).

**Deposit types**: skarn (contact metamorphic, common host for scheelite specifically), quartz-vein (classic wolframite-bearing veins, often associated with granite cupolas — historically significant in places like Cornwall, and in parts of Southeast Asia/China), and greisen-hosted.

**Grade reporting**: tungsten grade is conventionally reported as % WO₃ (tungsten trioxide) regardless of whether the mineral is wolframite ((Fe,Mn)WO₄) or scheelite (CaWO₄) — always check which mineral(s) carry the WO₃, since it directly determines the processing route (see below) and the deposit type context (quartz-vein/greisen for wolframite vs. skarn for scheelite, as a general tendency, though mixed-mineralogy deposits exist).

**Processing (wolframite route)**: gravity concentration (jigging, shaking tables, spirals) is the traditional and still common route for wolframite, since it has a high density contrast with gangue minerals — generally lower-capex than the flotation route more typical of scheelite. Magnetic separation is also used to help separate wolframite from other heavy minerals given its paramagnetic property.

**Market factors**: tungsten, like molybdenum and niobium, trades via producer/merchant contracts referencing specialist trade-publication price assessments (e.g., APT — ammonium paratungstate — pricing) rather than a major public exchange; check the study's price source and date rather than assuming a generic "tungsten price." Demand is driven by cemented carbide (cutting tools, mining/drilling bits) — a genuinely different end-use profile from most other metals in this reference set, so demand-cycle assumptions should be checked against industrial/machine-tool activity, not construction or EV narratives.

**Common evaluation pitfalls**: not distinguishing wolframite vs. scheelite mineralogy (they need different flowsheets and cost assumptions); assuming a liquid, transparent tungsten price benchmark exists when it doesn't; underweighting the geopolitical/critical-minerals angle to project financing and offtake for tungsten projects outside China.
